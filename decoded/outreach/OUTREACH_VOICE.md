## The Five-Part Structure

### 1. THANKFUL (+ AI Disclosure)
Open with genuine, specific gratitude for their research contribution. Reference a specific finding, framing, or methodological approach they used — not generic praise. End this paragraph (or weave it in) with an honest AI disclosure: explain that The Decoded Human uses AI to map connections across the literature, and that's how this email came to be written.

> **What good looks like:** "Your comprehensive review on antinutrients' contradictory roles caught my attention — specifically how you mapped the tension between nutrient inhibition and genuine bioactive longevity benefits. That's a nuanced argument to make rigorously, and you did it well. Full disclosure: I run The Decoded Human, a literature connectome that uses AI to trace connections across biomedical research — and that's precisely how this email came to be written."

### 2. IMPACT
Show where their work is making an impact in the broader research landscape. Be concrete about what their paper adds to the field. This should feel like a peer who actually read the paper, not a summary of the abstract.

> **What good looks like:** "Your work adds something the field genuinely needed: a systematic framework for holding both sides of the antinutrient story simultaneously, rather than defaulting to either demonization or uncritical enthusiasm. That balanced, mechanistic framing is what makes it citable across multiple disciplines."

### 3. WHY IT MATTERS
Connect their findings to patients, clinical translation, or fundamental biological understanding. This is where the neurosurgeon voice comes in: grounded, clinically aware, not abstract. No hype — just a scientist recognizing why this matters.

> **What good looks like:** "As a neurosurgeon who follows aging biology closely, what strikes me is how your findings on autophagy and cellular senescence as potential mediators of antinutrient benefits plug directly into ongoing debates about dietary modulation of the aging process — not abstractly, but with real implications for how we counsel patients about long-term diet."

### 4. THE CONNECTION
Present the specific AI-discovered connection. Name the connected paper. State the connection type clearly. Be intellectually honest about what this is: an AI observation at a stated confidence level, not a peer-reviewed finding. Include the link to thedecodedhuman.com/connections.

> **What good looks like:** "The AI flagged a convergence worth sharing: your paper shares four conceptual nodes — autophagy, cancer, cellular senescence, and related mechanisms — with *'The tight bond between Fanconi anemia and aging,'* which maps how FA recapitulates primary aging hallmarks including genomic instability and dysregulated autophagy. You can see how these papers connect here: https://thedecodedhuman.com/connections?c={connection_id}. To be transparent, this is an AI-generated observation at 70% confidence, not a peer-reviewed finding."

### 5. WHY IT'S IMPORTANT (+ Soft CTA)
Explain what this connection could mean for future research. What questions does it open? Close with a genuine, soft call-to-action: "I'd love to hear your thoughts on this." Not a sales pitch, not a request — genuine scientific curiosity.

> **What good looks like:** "What I find genuinely interesting: if dietary antinutrients modulate autophagy and senescence, could they intersect with the same pathways that accelerate aging in FA? That question wasn't visible before this mapping. I'd love to hear your thoughts on this."

## Tone Principles

- **Collegial, not authoritative.** Researcher-to-researcher, not expert-to-subject.
- **Warm but substantive.** Not marketing. Not flattery. Real scientific engagement.
- **Honest about AI.** Disclose in paragraph 1. State confidence levels. Never claim more than the AI found.
- **Clinically grounded.** Eric is a neurosurgeon. That lens should show — patient implications, clinical translation, why this matters at the bedside.
- **Never marketing.** No asks in the first email beyond their thoughts. No pitches. No "I'd love to collaborate" — too forward.
- **Specific, not generic.** Every sentence that praises their work must reference something specific they did. Generic flattery is the fastest way to lose credibility.

---

## Formatting Rules

- **Length:** 3–5 paragraphs, under 300 words total (body only, excluding sign-off and footer)
- **No bullet points.** Conversational prose only.
- **No em-dashes overuse.** Use them sparingly.
- **Subject line:** Specific to this paper and connection. Not generic. Should include either the paper's topic or the author's name + connection topic. Example: *"Your antinutrient dual-role findings and an unexpected connection to Fanconi anemia aging hallmarks"*
- **Sign-off:**
  ```
  Warm regards,
  Dr. Eric Whitney, DO
  drericwhitney@gmail.com
  https://thedecodedhuman.com
  ```
- **Footer (required, exact):**
  ```
  ---

  ```

## Prohibited Language

Never use these words/phrases:
- "game-changing", "revolutionary", "groundbreaking", "paradigm shift"
- "I'd love to collaborate" or any collaboration ask in first email
- "MD" (Eric is DO)
- Generic: "Your work is important" without specifying why
